## 1. What changed on the website

The homepage was redesigned. **The information is all the same** — same case
results, same testimonials, same FAQ, same phone numbers. It's organised
differently and it loads faster.

Three things you'll actually notice:

**The homepage now leads with the $543,000 case.** The one where the defense
offered $5,000 and the jury awarded $543,000. It's drawn as two bars, to scale,
so you can see the difference. It's the first thing under the headline.

*Why this matters to you:* clients will mention it. It's a good opening — "that's
a real Prince George's County case, and it's why we don't take the first offer."

**The Google reviews section moved way up**, right under the case results. It
shows the top-rated reviews and the most recent ones, and you can switch between
them.

**There's a "Leave a review" button everywhere**, and it now walks a client
through it properly. Details in §3.

## 2. Why reviews matter this much

Someone was just in a car accident. They're in pain, they're scared, and they're
searching "personal injury lawyer near me" on a cracked phone screen.

They see five firms. Every one of them says "125 years of experience" and
"millions recovered." **They cannot tell you apart from the copy.**

What they do next is read the reviews. Not the ratings — the *words*. They're
looking for someone who sounds like them, who had the problem they have.

That's why a review that says:

> "Chris walked me through every step of my personal injury case, and Donna the
> paralegal kept me informed and answered all my questions."

is worth more than a hundred bare five-star clicks. It's specific. It has names
in it. It tells the next person what it actually feels like to be your client.

**Every review you help a happy client leave is a client who finds you next
month.** That's the whole mechanism.

## 3. How a client leaves a review now

They tap **Leave a review** anywhere on the site. A panel slides up.

### Step 1 — They pick their office

Waldorf, Lanham, Frederick, or Rockville.

**This is the fix for our biggest problem.** Each office has its own separate
Google listing. Before, a client had to search Google, find the right listing out
of four near-identical ones, and hope. Reviews landed on the wrong office, or
got abandoned halfway.

Now they tap their office and go straight to the right place.

> **You need to know:** if a client reviews the wrong office, **we cannot move
> it.** Google won't transfer reviews between listings. It stays where it landed
> forever. So if you're helping someone in person — make sure they pick the
> office that actually handled their case.

### Step 2 — Prompts, if they're stuck

Four reminder questions:

- Who did you work with?
- What were you dealing with?
- How did they keep you informed?
- What would you tell a friend?

**These are prompts, not scripts.** There's no pre-written review to copy. That's
deliberate — see §6.

The reason they're there: most people who abandon a review don't refuse. They
open the box, stare at it, can't think what to say, and close it. A prompt fixes
that. It's the single biggest source of drop-off in the whole flow.

### Step 3 — Google opens

Tapping **Open the Google review box** takes them straight to Google with our
listing loaded and the stars ready. They pick stars, type a sentence, tap Post.

**They need a Google account.** Almost every Android user has one. Most iPhone
users do too (any Gmail address). If they genuinely don't, they'd need to make
one — and at that point it's usually kinder to stop asking.

### Step 4 — It appears

On our Google listing right away, and on the website within about a day.

### On a computer? There's a QR code.

If a client is on a laptop, the panel shows a QR code. They scan it with their
phone and finish there.

**Use this at the office.** Someone signing final paperwork at your desk, on your
screen — turn the monitor, they scan, they review before they've left the
building. That's the highest-conversion moment you will ever get.

## 4. When to ask

**The moment of relief.** There's a specific point where a client goes from
stressed to grateful. That's your window. It's usually:

| Moment | Why it works |
|---|---|
| **The settlement check clears** | The single best moment. It's over, it worked, they're relieved. |
| **Right after a good call** — "we settled" | Emotion is highest. Ask while you're still on the phone. |
| **Final paperwork in the office** | They're physically here. QR code. Done in 90 seconds. |
| **They thank you, unprompted** | They've handed you the opening. Take it. |
| **They say "I'd recommend you to anyone"** | That is a review, said out loud. Ask them to type it. |

**Don't ask:**

- Mid-case, before there's an outcome
- Right after bad news
- When they're frustrated about timing or the process
- More than twice, ever

> **Ask once. Follow up once. Then stop.** A third ask makes people feel used and
> costs you the goodwill you just earned. It's not worth it.

## 5. How to ask — scripts

Adapt these. Don't read them.

### On the phone, right after good news

> "Before you go — I'm glad this worked out. Would you mind leaving us a Google
> review? It takes about thirty seconds, and honestly it's how most people find
> us. I can text you a link right now while we're talking."

**Then text it immediately.** While you're still on the call. A link sent an hour
later gets opened maybe one time in ten. Sent while they're on the phone with
you, it gets opened while they're on the phone with you.

### In the office, final paperwork

> "One last thing while you're here — would you leave us a Google review? Here,
> scan this with your phone, it'll take you right to it."

Turn the screen. Let them scan. **Don't watch them type** — hand them the space
and go get their copies.

### By email or text, after the check clears

> Hi [Name],
>
> Now that everything's wrapped up, I wanted to say it was genuinely good working
> with you.
>
> If you have a minute, would you leave us a Google review? A sentence or two
> about your experience helps the next person in your situation figure out who to
> call.
>
> [link]
>
> Thanks either way.
> [Your name]

**Why "thanks either way" is in there:** it removes the pressure. Counterintuitively,
that raises the response rate. People help when they're asked, not when they're
squeezed.

### When they say "I don't know what to write"

This is the most common objection, and it's the easiest one.

> "Just whatever comes to mind — who you worked with, what you were dealing with,
> whether we kept you in the loop. Two sentences is plenty. There are some
> reminder prompts on the page if you get stuck."

**Never offer to write it for them.** See §6.

## 6. The rules you cannot break

Read this section twice. These aren't office preferences. Breaking any of them
can get our Google listing suspended — and our listing is where a large share of
new clients come from.

### ❌ Never write a review for a client

Not a draft. Not "here's something to start with." Not "just copy this and change
whatever you want."

It's against Google's policy, it's dishonest, and it's obvious — reviews written
by staff read like staff wrote them. Prospective clients can tell, and it does
more damage than no review at all.

**Prompts are fine. Words in their mouth are not.**

### ❌ Never offer anything in exchange

No discounts, no gift cards, no raffle entries, no "we'll waive the copy fee." Not
even coffee.

Incentivised reviews are prohibited. It also makes every genuine review we have
look purchased, and for a law firm it raises fee and conduct issues nobody wants
to explain.

### ❌ Never ask only the happy ones

**This is the one people get wrong with the best intentions.**

You cannot screen clients before asking — no "were you satisfied? Yes → here's
the Google link. No → here's a feedback form."

It's called **review gating**. Google explicitly prohibits it. Penalties run from
having reviews removed to the listing being suspended.

Notice the website doesn't do this either. Everyone who opens that panel gets the
same Google link. The "tell us privately" option sits *next to* it, offered to
everyone. That's the design, on purpose, and it's why we're safe.

**Ask everyone you'd be comfortable asking. Ask them the same way.**

### ❌ Never use the office computer or wifi to post

Multiple reviews from one IP address is a spam signal. Google will filter them —
including the real ones. Hand them their own phone.

### ❌ Never ask someone who isn't a client

No family, no friends, no vendors, no staff. Fake reviews are the fastest way to
lose a listing, and they're detectable.

### ✅ What you can always do

- Ask any real client, once, at a good moment
- Text or email them the link
- Show them the QR code
- Point at the prompts if they freeze
- Say thank you

## 7. Handling an unhappy client

Sometimes you'll ask and get "actually, I wasn't thrilled with how long it took."

**Good. That's useful, and you just found out for free.**

**Do:**
- Listen. Don't defend. Let them finish.
- "I'm sorry — that's not the experience we want. Tell me what happened."
- Fix it if it's fixable
- Tell them about the private feedback option — "if you want that to go straight
  to the managing partner, there's a link on our site"
- Escalate internally

**Don't:**
- Talk them out of leaving a review
- Suggest they leave the private feedback *instead of* a Google review — that's
  gating (§6), even said casually and kindly
- Argue about the facts
- Promise anything you can't deliver

**If they leave a negative review anyway:** that's their right. It goes to
whoever handles review responses (§8). One thoughtful three-star among fifty
fives is not a crisis — it actually makes the fives look real. A wall of nothing
but perfect fives is what makes people suspicious.

## 8. Responding to reviews

Only whoever is designated does this. But everyone should know the standard.

**Every review gets a response.** Positive ones too. Prospective clients read the
responses — it's how they judge whether you're paying attention.

**Positive:** short, specific, warm. Name the person they mentioned.
> "Thank you, Richard. Chris and Donna will be glad to hear this. Best of luck to
> you."

**Negative:** this is the one that matters, and there's one absolute rule.

> ### ⚠️ Never confirm that someone was a client.
>
> Not "we're sorry your case didn't go as hoped." Not "our records show." Not
> even "thank you for choosing us."
>
> **Confirming a client relationship in a public reply is a confidentiality
> breach.** State bars have disciplined lawyers for exactly this — responding to
> a bad review by explaining their side. The client waived nothing by posting.
> You waive plenty by answering.

**The only safe shape:**
> "Thank you for the feedback. We take concerns like this seriously and would
> like to understand what happened. Please call our office at (301) 932-9997 and
> ask for [name]."

Nothing about the case. Nothing about whether they were a client. Move it
offline. **Attorney review before posting any response to a negative review.**

## 9. Questions clients ask

**"Do I need a Google account?"**
Yes. If you have Gmail or an Android phone, you already have one.

**"Will they see my name?"**
Yes — whatever name is on their Google account, and their profile photo. There's
no anonymous option. Be upfront; don't let them find out after.

**"Can I change it later?"**
Yes. They can edit or delete their own review any time.

**"Can you take it down if I change my mind?"**
No. We have no control over it at all. Only they can remove it. Say this plainly
— it's the honest answer and it builds trust.

**"Which office do I pick?"**
The one that handled their case. If they're unsure, check the file. It can't be
moved later.

**"How long does it take?"**
About thirty seconds. Two sentences is plenty.

**"I'd rather not."**
> "No problem at all — thanks anyway."

Then drop it. Completely. Don't circle back.

## 10. Quick reference

### The ask, in one line
> "Would you leave us a Google review? Takes thirty seconds, and it's how most
> people find us. I'll text you the link right now."

### Best moments
Check clears · "We settled" call · Final paperwork in-office · Any unprompted thank-you

### Never
Write it for them · Offer anything for it · Screen for happy clients first · Use office wifi · Ask non-clients · Ask three times

### Always
Ask once, follow up once · Text the link *while on the phone* · QR code in the office · Right office, it can't be moved · Thank them either way

### Responding to a bad review
**Never confirm they were a client.** Thank them, offer a phone number, attorney
reviews before it posts.

### Escalate to
- Unhappy client → case manager, then managing partner
- Negative review → whoever handles responses, **attorney review before posting**
- Review on the wrong office → nothing can be done, but flag it so we know
- Website broken → your web contact
